Your arguments are undercut by repeated use of hedging language throughout the speech, which weakens the authority of your case.

• 
Cut the hedging words
: Phrases like "I think," "I'm pretty sure," and "maybe" signal uncertainty to the audience and opposition — just make the claim directly and confidently.

• 
End with conclusiveness
: Your speech trails off mid-sentence; you need a strong, definitive closing statement that reinforces your side's position and leaves the judges with a clear final impression.
• 
Hedging undermines your authority
: You say things like "I think this is quite intuitive" and "I'm pretty sure that over a period of time, I will be more healthier" — these phrases invite the opposition to disagree. Drop the qualifier and just assert the claim.
  • Original: "I'm pretty sure that over a period of time, I will be more healthier"

"Maybe" signals uncertainty
: You say "maybe water, maybe juice" when describing what people will switch to — in a debate, this reads as not knowing your own argument. Name the alternative confidently.

  • Alternative: "they switch to healthier alternatives — water, juice, drinks that actually hydrate."

Vague filler weakens your logic chain
: The phrase "yada yada yada" appears mid-argument when you're explaining the domino effect of sugary drink consumption. This is a key moment in your reasoning — replacing it with the actual next step in the chain would make the argument far more persuasive.

• 
Your Rule of Three structure is strong but inconsistent

Consider making them parallel and concise, like: "body, values, markets" — short, sequential, and memorable.

Your conclusion is incomplete
: The speech ends mid-sentence — "because we care about those" — with no summary, no call to action, and no closing statement. A strong conclusion should briefly restate your three impacts and end with a definitive, confident line that signals the round is closed on your terms.
