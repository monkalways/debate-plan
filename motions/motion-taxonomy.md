# Motion Taxonomy

The backbone of the library. Before building a case, **identify the motion type** —
it tells you your burden, what you must prove, and which mechanism family to reach for.
Mis-reading the type is the most common way good debaters lose: they prove the wrong thing.

> **How to use under the clock:** Read the motion → name the type (5 sec) → load its
> burden + mechanism family → start the 4-link chains. The type does half your framing
> for you.

---

## Quick identifier

| Stem | Type | You must prove… |
|------|------|-----------------|
| **This House Would / Should** (THW) | **Policy** | the action produces net benefit |
| **THW ban / abolish / criminalise** | **Abolition** | the thing is harmful *and* removing it is better than reforming it |
| **This House Believes That** (THBT) | **Value / Analysis** | the judgment is true (often no actor/action) |
| **This House Regrets** (THR) | **Regret** | the world is worse *because of* the thing vs. a realistic alternative |
| **This House Prefers / would choose** (THP) | **Comparative** | your option beats the *other named option* (not the status quo) |
| **This House Supports / Opposes** (THS) | **Stance** | the movement/actor is, on balance, justified |
| **TH, as [actor], would…** | **Actor** | the action serves *that actor's* goals and values |
| **X has done more harm than good** | **Balance** | the harms outweigh the goods (a weighing motion by design) |

---

## 1. Policy — THW / THS (This House Would…)
**Asks:** Should this action be taken? *Example: THW impose a carbon tax.*
- **Prop burden:** the policy is *workable* and produces net benefit vs. the status quo.
- **Opp burden:** the harms outweigh, OR the status quo / a better alternative is preferable. Opp does **not** have to defend doing nothing — a counter-policy is allowed.
- **Key moves:** mechanism (how the policy actually causes the good), stakeholder analysis (who's affected), and a realistic comparison to what happens *without* it (your ¬a→¬b→¬c chain *is* this).
- **Mechanism families:** market failure, incentive distortion, public goods, coordination.
- **Trap:** don't just assert benefits — show the *causal path*. Opp's best attack is "your mechanism won't fire."

## 2. Abolition — THW ban / abolish
**Asks:** Should we eliminate this entirely? *Example: THW ban private schools.*
- **Prop burden:** higher than ordinary policy — the thing is harmful **and** an outright ban beats *reform/regulation*. You must beat the milder alternative, not just the status quo.
- **Opp's strongest line:** "regulate, don't ban" — concede the harm, offer the gentler fix.
- **Key move (Prop):** prove the harm is *intrinsic* (can't be reformed away) and that workarounds won't just replace it.
- **Mechanism families:** capture/accountability, systemic discrimination, externality.
- **Trap:** Prop loses if Opp's "reform instead" captures most of the benefit at less cost.

## 3. Value / Analysis — THBT
**Asks:** Is this judgment true? Often **no policy, no actor** — just an evaluation. *Example: THBT capitalism cannot solve the climate crisis.*
- **Burden:** establish a **measure** ("good debater = wins ballots"; "harm = …") and prove your side meets it. Whoever sets the better measure usually wins.
- **Key move:** define the standard early and characterise the world; these are argued through *comparison and weighing*, not policy mechanisms.
- **Trap:** don't drift into "what should we do" — there's no action to take. Stay on the *truth of the claim*.

## 4. Regret — THR (This House Regrets X)
**Asks:** Would the world be better if X had not happened? *Example: THR the rise of cancel culture.*
- **Burden:** **counterfactual** — compare the world *with* X to the realistic world *without* X (not a fantasy world). The harm must be *because of* X.
- **Key move:** this is the 4-link chain's natural home — the ¬a→¬b→¬c side is the whole debate. Establish the counterfactual carefully; Opp wins by showing the alternative was just as bad or worse.
- **Trap:** don't compare to utopia. Compare to what *realistically* would have happened instead.

## 5. Comparative — THP (This House Prefers A to B)
**Asks:** Which of two named options is better? *Example: THP idealistic leaders to pragmatic ones.*
- **Burden:** beat the **other option**, not the status quo. The status quo is irrelevant.
- **Key move:** every argument must be *comparative* — "ours delivers more of X, theirs less." Weighing isn't a closing step here; it's the whole case.
- **Trap:** running standalone benefits of your side without comparing to theirs. A benefit both options share is worth zero.

## 6. Stance — THS / TH Opposes (This House Supports X)
**Asks:** Is this movement/actor/idea, on balance, justified? *Example: THS the use of civil disobedience.*
- **Burden:** the thing is *net justified* given its aims and methods. Often values-heavy (means vs. ends).
- **Key move:** characterise the actor fairly, weigh principle (rights, justice) against consequences.
- **Mechanism families:** rights (autonomy, harm principle), legitimacy.

## 7. Actor — TH, as [actor], would…
**Asks:** What should *this specific actor* do, given *their* goals? *Example: TH, as the EU, would admit Ukraine.*
- **Burden:** argue from **the actor's interests and values**, not abstract right/wrong. What advances *their* position?
- **Key move:** step fully into the actor's shoes — their incentives, constraints, reputation, rivals.
- **Trap:** arguing what's good for the world instead of what's good for the actor. Wrong lens = wrong debate.

## 8. Balance — "X has done more harm than good"
**Asks:** Do the harms outweigh the goods? *Example: THBT the smartphone has done more harm than good.*
> **Overlap with Value/THBT:** these are *phrased* as THBT, but the **measure is handed to you** (harm vs. good), so the weighing IS the burden. When a THBT has "more harm than good" baked in, treat it as **Balance**, not open Value.
- **Burden:** explicitly **weigh** — this motion is a weighing contest by design. Both sides will have real points; the winner *compares* them (scope, magnitude, reversibility).
- **Key move:** don't just list your side's effects — rank them against the other side's. Concede small to win big.
- **Trap:** a pile of harms with no weighing loses to a smaller pile that's been *weighed*.

## The one habit for every type
Whatever the type, end every argument by answering **"so what, and why does this beat
their side?"** — the comparative/weighing move. (See the Weighing & Clash track in the
plan.) The motion type tells you *what* to prove; weighing tells the judge *why you win*.

---

## Week 1 drill — the 90-second Type Drill
Make classification automatic — this is the *input* that makes prep fast. Daily:
1. Pull **8 motions** off a list.
2. For each, say out loud, on a timer: its **type** + the **one thing you must prove**.
3. Goal: all 8 in **under 90 seconds**, no hesitation. Once it's automatic, prep time
   freed by instant classification goes straight into substance and weighing.
