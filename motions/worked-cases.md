# Worked Motion Cases

Full cases for specific motions. Each argument uses the 4-link counterfactual chain:
- **a → b** because… (passing the motion triggers the mechanism)
- **b → c** because… (the mechanism produces the impact)
- **¬a → ¬b** because… (without the motion, no mechanism)
- **¬b → ¬c** because… (without the mechanism, no impact)

Where `a` = the motion, `b` = that argument's mechanism, `c` = its impact.
Add a **weighing sentence** after each argument ("this outweighs their case because…").

# Motion 1: This House would impose a carbon tax

## PROPOSITION

### P1 — Internalizing the externality cuts emissions
- **a→b:** A carbon tax → polluters bear the cost of emissions, because every tonne carries a direct, legally enforced price.
- **b→c:** Polluters bearing the cost → emissions fall, because clean alternatives become comparatively cheaper and firms switch to cut their tax bill.
- **¬a→¬b:** No carbon tax → polluters bear no cost, because pollution stays a free externality dumped on society.
- **¬b→¬c:** No cost borne → emissions don't fall, because polluting stays cheapest and no firm cuts voluntarily while rivals don't.

### P2 — Revenue funds the green transition
- **a→b:** A carbon tax → government gains a large new revenue stream, because it collects on every unit of pollution across the economy.
- **b→c:** Green revenue → clean infrastructure gets built, because funds can go to renewables, transit, and rebates for the poor.
- **¬a→¬b:** No carbon tax → no dedicated green revenue, because nothing ties funds to emissions.
- **¬b→¬c:** No green revenue → the transition stalls, because clean infrastructure is costly and competes with other budget priorities.

### P3 — The price signal drives innovation
- **a→b:** A carbon tax → firms gain an ongoing incentive to innovate, because cutting emissions directly cuts their costs year after year.
- **b→c:** Innovation incentive → cleaner technology develops, because firms invest in R&D to escape the tax.
- **¬a→¬b:** No carbon tax → no ongoing incentive to innovate, because there's no reward for emitting less.
- **¬b→¬c:** No innovation incentive → clean tech lags, because R&D follows profit and dirty tech stays cheaper.

## OPPOSITION

### O1 — It is regressive and hits the poor hardest
- **a→b:** A carbon tax → the price of energy and goods rises, because firms pass the tax on to consumers.
- **b→c:** Rising prices → the poor are disproportionately harmed, because energy and transport eat a far larger share of low incomes.
- **¬a→¬b:** No carbon tax → energy prices don't rise from it, because there's no added cost to pass on.
- **¬b→¬c:** Prices stable → the poor aren't squeezed, because their essential costs hold steady.

### O2 — Carbon leakage: firms just relocate
- **a→b:** A carbon tax → domestic production becomes costlier, because firms face a cost their foreign competitors don't.
- **b→c:** Higher domestic cost → firms relocate abroad, so emissions and jobs simply move to untaxed (often dirtier) countries.
- **¬a→¬b:** No carbon tax → domestic firms aren't disadvantaged, because they face the same costs as rivals.
- **¬b→¬c:** No disadvantage → firms stay, because emissions stay accountable at home and jobs stay put.

### O3 — Better, targeted alternatives exist
- **a→b:** A carbon tax → policy locks onto one blunt tool, because political capital and revenue get committed to the tax approach.
- **b→c:** One blunt tool → worse outcomes than targeted policy, because direct investment in renewables and regulation of big polluters cut emissions faster without taxing the poor.
- **¬a→¬b:** No carbon tax → policy keeps flexibility, because resources aren't locked into a single mechanism.
- **¬b→¬c:** Flexible policy → better-targeted cuts, because government can fund clean energy and regulate top emitters directly.

# Motion 2: This House would ban private schools

## PROPOSITION

### P1 — Equality of opportunity
- **a→b:** Banning private schools → wealthy families' children enter the public system, because there's no paid alternative to opt into.
- **b→c:** Rich kids in public schools → public education improves for all, because powerful, engaged parents now demand quality where everyone benefits.
- **¬a→¬b:** No ban → the wealthy stay in private schools, because they can buy a better option.
- **¬b→¬c:** The wealthy opting out → public schools stay under-resourced, because those with the most political power have no stake in them.

### P2 — Breaks the cycle of elite reproduction
- **a→b:** Banning private schools → elite university and job access depends less on purchased advantage, because credentials no longer track ability to pay.
- **b→c:** Less purchased advantage → social mobility rises, because talent rather than wealth decides who advances.
- **¬a→¬b:** No ban → purchased advantage persists, because the rich buy superior schooling, tutoring, and connections.
- **¬b→¬c:** Persistent advantage → mobility stays frozen, because each generation's wealth converts into the next generation's opportunities.

### P3 — Reallocates the best teachers and resources
- **a→b:** Banning private schools → top teachers and funding flow into the public system, because that's the only place left to work and enroll.
- **b→c:** Better teachers/funding in public schools → overall quality rises, because the best resources serve the many instead of the few.
- **¬a→¬b:** No ban → top resources stay siphoned into private schools, because they pay more and select easier students.
- **¬b→¬c:** Resources siphoned away → public quality lags, because the system serving most children is starved of the best inputs.

## OPPOSITION

### O1 — Violates parental freedom
- **a→b:** Banning private schools → the state forces all families into one system, because it strips their right to choose how their child is educated.
- **b→c:** Forcing one system → parental liberty is violated, because deeply personal choices about a child's values are seized by the state.
- **¬a→¬b:** No ban → families keep the choice, because the private option remains.
- **¬b→¬c:** Families keeping choice → liberty is preserved, because parents decide what fits their child.

### O2 — Overwhelms public capacity
- **a→b:** Banning private schools → millions of students flood the public system at once, because every private student must enroll publicly.
- **b→c:** Flooding the system → quality drops for everyone, because the same resources are stretched across far more students.
- **¬a→¬b:** No ban → private schools absorb their share of students, because they keep operating.
- **¬b→¬c:** Private schools absorbing students → public class sizes stay manageable, because the system isn't overwhelmed.

### O3 — The wealthy just find workarounds
- **a→b:** Banning private schools → the wealthy switch to private tutors, elite neighborhoods, and homeschooling, because they'll always convert money into advantage.
- **b→c:** New workarounds → inequality persists anyway, because advantage moves to less visible, less regulated channels.
- **¬a→¬b:** No ban → advantage stays concentrated in schools, because that's where the wealthy currently spend.
- **¬b→¬c:** Advantage staying in schools → it's at least visible and regulable, because the state can set curriculum and standards there, unlike private tutoring.

# Motion 3: This House would lower the voting age to 16

## PROPOSITION

### P1 — Stakeholders deserve a voice
- **a→b:** Lowering the voting age → 16-year-olds gain a say in policy, because they can now cast a ballot.
- **b→c:** 16-year-olds voting → policy better reflects their interests, because politicians must court their votes on education and climate that affect them most.
- **¬a→¬b:** No change → 16-year-olds have no say, because they can't vote.
- **¬b→¬c:** No say → their interests are ignored, because politicians have no electoral reason to serve a group that can't vote.

### P2 — Builds lifelong voting habits
- **a→b:** Lowering the voting age → teens first vote while in school and at home, because they're in a stable, structured environment.
- **b→c:** Voting in a stable setting → lifelong turnout rises, because voting is habit-forming and a first vote in supportive surroundings sticks.
- **¬a→¬b:** No change → first voting happens at 18, amid moving, college, or work, because that's when eligibility begins.
- **¬b→¬c:** First vote in an unstable period → the habit doesn't form, because disruption makes that first vote easy to skip.

### P3 — 16-year-olds are competent enough
- **a→b:** Lowering the voting age → society treats 16-year-olds as civic participants, because the law recognizes their judgment.
- **b→c:** Recognized as participants → civic engagement deepens, because schools and families take political development seriously when it counts.
- **¬a→¬b:** No change → 16-year-olds are treated as politically irrelevant, because their views carry no electoral weight.
- **¬b→¬c:** Treated as irrelevant → engagement stays shallow, because there's no real stake driving them to learn.

## OPPOSITION

### O1 — Lack of maturity and experience
- **a→b:** Lowering the voting age → less experienced voters enter the electorate, because 16-year-olds have less life experience and independence.
- **b→c:** Less experienced electorate → worse collective decisions, because votes are cast with less grasp of long-term consequences.
- **¬a→¬b:** No change → the electorate keeps its experience baseline, because the threshold stays at 18.
- **¬b→¬c:** Experienced baseline → more considered decisions, because voters carry more real-world stake and knowledge.

### O2 — Vulnerable to undue influence
- **a→b:** Lowering the voting age → teens vote while under daily authority of parents and teachers, because they're enfranchised while still at home and school.
- **b→c:** Voting under authority → their votes are unduly influenced, because they're more susceptible to pressure from those above them.
- **¬a→¬b:** No change → people vote once more independent, because they start at 18 when many have left home.
- **¬b→¬c:** More independent voters → freer choices, because they're less subject to direct daily authority.

### O3 — Breaks the consistency of legal adulthood
- **a→b:** Lowering the voting age → voting is split from other adult thresholds, because society still bars 16-year-olds from contracts, military service, and drinking.
- **b→c:** Splitting voting from adulthood → the line of responsibility becomes incoherent, because society can't justify "mature enough to vote, too immature for all else."
- **¬a→¬b:** No change → voting stays aligned with legal adulthood, because 18 marks a consistent bundle of rights.
- **¬b→¬c:** Aligned thresholds → a coherent, defensible standard, because rights and responsibilities arrive together.

# Motion 4: THW implement a universal basic income (UBI)
*(Economics · Week 1)*

## PROPOSITION

**P1 — A guaranteed income floor ends absolute poverty**
- **a→b:** UBI → everyone gets a guaranteed income floor, because the state pays a regular unconditional sum.
- **b→c:** Income floor → absolute poverty ends, because no one can fall below subsistence.
- **¬a→¬b:** No UBI → gaps in the safety net persist, because means-tested welfare misses people through stigma, bureaucracy, and eligibility gaps.
- **¬b→¬c:** Gaps persist → poverty continues, because the most vulnerable slip through the cracks.

**P2 — It gives workers bargaining power**
- **a→b:** UBI → workers can refuse exploitative jobs, because they're no longer one paycheck from ruin.
- **b→c:** Bargaining power → wages and conditions improve, because employers must compete to attract labour.
- **¬a→¬b:** No UBI → workers must accept any job, because survival depends on the next wage.
- **¬b→¬c:** No bargaining power → exploitation continues, because desperate workers can't say no.

**P3 — It is simpler than means-tested welfare**
- **a→b:** UBI → the welfare bureaucracy shrinks, because a universal payment needs no eligibility testing.
- **b→c:** Less bureaucracy → more help reaches people at lower cost, because admin overhead and errors fall.
- **¬a→¬b:** No UBI → complex means-testing remains, because every programme must police who qualifies.
- **¬b→¬c:** Complex system → waste and exclusion, because admin costs and mistakes deny people aid they're owed.

## OPPOSITION

**O1 — It's unaffordable and crowds out targeted help**
- **a→b:** UBI → an enormous universal bill, because everyone, including the rich, gets paid.
- **b→c:** Huge cost → taxes soar or other services are cut, because the money must come from somewhere.
- **¬a→¬b:** No UBI → spending stays targeted, because money goes only to those who need it.
- **¬b→¬c:** Targeted spending → more help per dollar for the poor, because nothing is spent on people who don't need it.

**O2 — It weakens the incentive to work**
- **a→b:** UBI → some people work less, because income no longer depends on working.
- **b→c:** Less work → lower output and a shrinking tax base, because fewer people are producing.
- **¬a→¬b:** No UBI → work stays necessary for income, because there's no unconditional floor.
- **¬b→¬c:** Work necessary → labour supply holds, because people must earn to live.
> *Weakest link:* Prop attacks link 1 — real pilots show only small work-reduction effects, mostly among students and new parents. Defend by citing specific trials.

**O3 — Inflation erodes the benefit**
- **a→b:** UBI → everyone holds more cash, because all receive the payment.
- **b→c:** More cash chasing the same goods → prices rise, especially rent, because demand outstrips fixed supply.
- **¬a→¬b:** No UBI → no broad cash injection, because support is smaller and targeted.
- **¬b→¬c:** No injection → prices stay stable, because aggregate demand doesn't spike.
