# Framing & Style

The toolkit for **"writing fluently and interestingly" (weakness, 5/10)** and for the
intro/signposting of every speech. Style doesn't replace substance — it *delivers* it.
A judge remembers the team that made them *see* the argument.

> ⚠️ **Scaffolds, not scripts** (same rule as the mechanism bank). These are patterns to
> adapt in your own voice. A memorised intro sounds memorised — and that costs you.

> **How to use:** in the speed-write, the **intro + roadmap** is the last 2 minutes.
> Pull a frame from here, drop in the motion, go. The rhetorical devices are for the
> Saturday style-rewrite — take one argument and make it *vivid*.

---

## 1. The speech skeleton (World Schools, ~6 min)
Every constructive speech has the same bones. Signpost each one out loud.

```
1. HOOK + FRAME      (~30s)  — why this debate matters; the lens to judge it through
2. (rebuttal)        (varies)— 2nd/3rd speakers engage the other side first
3. ROADMAP           (~15s)  — "I have two arguments: first…, second…"
4. ARGUMENT 1        (~2m)   — claim → 4-link mechanism → example → weighing
5. ARGUMENT 2        (~2m)   — same structure
6. CRYSTALLISE       (~30s)  — the one comparison the judge should vote on
```

**Speaker roles (so he knows what each speech is *for*):**
- **1st:** define + framework + your two best constructive arguments.
- **2nd:** rebuild against their rebuttal + your remaining argument(s). Rebuttal-heavy.
- **3rd:** mostly **rebuttal + weighing** — *no new arguments*; show why you've won the clashes.
- **Reply (~4 min):** a biased summary — the story of why your side won. No new material.

---

## 2. Intros & framing
The first 30 seconds buy you the judge's attention. Four reliable frames:

**A. The stakes frame** — name who is hurt and how much.
> *"This debate is about [whose lives], and whether we let [harm] continue or finally act."*
> *Example (carbon tax):* "This is a debate about who pays for a burning planet — the
> polluters who profit, or the families who never chose this."

**B. The principle frame** — name the value at war.
> *"At its heart, this is a clash between [value A] and [value B]."*
> *Example (private schools):* "This is a fight between one family's freedom to buy
> advantage and every child's right to a fair start."

**C. The characterisation frame** — paint the person behind the policy.
> *"Picture [specific person] who [situation]…"*
> *Example (voting age):* "Picture a sixteen-year-old who works, pays tax, and watches
> decisions about her future made by people who never have to ask her."

**D. The reframe** (when the other side defined it narrowly) — *take back the question*.
> *"Our opponents want you to believe this debate is about X. It isn't. It's about Y."*

**Define + measure (1st speaker):** after the hook, set the standard.
> *"We'll show our side wins on [measure]: who is helped, how many, and how badly the
> alternative fails them."*

---

## 3. Signposting & roadmaps
Judges flow what they can follow. Be almost mechanically clear.

- **Roadmap:** *"Two arguments today: first, [label]; second, [label]."*
- **Signpost into each:** *"My first argument — [label]."*
- **Signpost the parts:** *"Here's the mechanism… here's why it matters…"*
- **Transitions:** *"That's why X happens. But it gets worse —"* / *"So far, [recap]. Now—"*
- **Label your arguments** with sticky names ("the ladder-pulling point", "the lock-in
  problem") so they're easy to extend and reference later.

---

## 4. The fluency toolkit (rhetorical devices)
This is the direct fix for the writing weakness. Use **one or two per speech**, not all —
over-spiced is as bad as bland.

| Device | What it does | Example |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| **Rule of three** | rhythm + memorability | "It's unfair, it's unworkable, and it's unkind." |
| **Contrast / antithesis** | sharpens the clash | "They protect profits; we protect people." |
| **Rhetorical question** | pulls the judge in | "Who actually pays when the factory pollutes?" |
| **Vivid concrete image** | makes it *seen* | "a child doing homework by candlelight" beats "energy poverty" |
| **Callback** | ties the speech together | open with an image, return to it at the end |
| **Anaphora** (repeat a phrase) | builds force | "We say no to… We say no to… We say no to…" |
| **The pivot** | concede small, win big | "Yes, it costs money. But what's the cost of doing nothing?" |
| **Naming the stake** | raises magnitude | "This isn't an inconvenience. It's a generation's future." |

**Show, don't tell:** replace abstractions with pictures.
- ❌ "There are negative economic consequences for low-income households."
- ✅ "A mother in that household now chooses between heating and groceries."

## 5. Making it vivid — characterisation
Vividness and *knowledge* reinforce each other (this also feeds the Knowledge weakness):
a real example, named and pictured, is both substance and style.
- Put a **face** on the impact: "the farmer", "the nurse", "the kid".
- Use a **real case or stat** from the examples bank, then humanise it.
- Scale it: from one person → to the many. ("Now multiply her by 800 million.")

---

## 6. Clash language (sound responsive)
Rebuttal lands harder with clean framing verbs:
- *"They claim X. There are three problems with that…"*
- *"Even if that's true, it doesn't matter, because…"* (the *even-if*)
- *"That's not responsive to our point, which was…"*
- *"They need X to be true. It isn't, because…"*
- *"They have no answer to…"* (only if they genuinely didn't)

## 7. Weighing language (why you win)
The ceiling skill — say these out loud at the end of arguments and in the 3rd/reply:
- *"Even if they win their point, we win the round, because we outweigh on…"*
- **Scope:** "ours affects more people." **Magnitude:** "ours is a bigger harm."
- **Probability:** "ours is certain; theirs is speculative." **Reversibility:** "theirs
  can't be undone." **Timeframe:** "ours hits now and lasts."
- *"At the end of this debate, the question you're really voting on is…"* (crystallise)

---

## 8. Conclusions
- Don't introduce anything new. **Crystallise** the one comparison.
- Land the callback from your intro.
- End on a clean, strong line — short. *"Vote for the side that put people first."*

---

## 9. Style drills (for the Style track)
- **Rewrite-for-vividness (Sat):** take one argument from a speed-write and rewrite it
  using *show-don't-tell* + one device. Read both aloud — hear the difference.
- **Intro reps:** given a random motion, write a 30-second hook in each of the 4 frames.
  Pick the strongest. 5 minutes.
- **Filler hunt:** play back a recording, count "um / like / basically". Track the number
  in the log; drive it down.
- **One-device focus:** pick a device of the week (e.g., rule of three) and force it into
  every practice speech until it's natural.

---

## 10. Style mistakes to avoid
- **Memorised-sounding delivery** — the #1 style killer for a confident speaker. Adapt, don't recite.
- **Over-decoration** — three rhetorical questions in a row is noise. One, placed well.
- **Abstraction** — "negative societal impacts" says nothing. Name the person.
- **Flat signposting voice** — vary pace and emphasis; land the key line slower.
- **Style without substance** — a beautiful sentence around an empty argument still loses.
  Style is the *wrapper*; the mechanism is the *gift*.
