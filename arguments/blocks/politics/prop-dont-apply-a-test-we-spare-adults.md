ARGUMENT: reject any bar imposed on the young that we never impose on adults

CLAIM: The "16-year-olds are easily led by their parents" objection fails, because the same test would disqualify half the adult electorate too — and the evidence says it isn't even true.

CHAIN:
  L1  Opp's disqualifier (undue influence) → applies to adults just as much
      Warrant: adults routinely vote on family, habit and vibes; if being influenced disqualifies you, "half the electorate would lose a ballot."
      Evidence: —
  L2  We don't strip adults for it → the test is being invented only for 16s
      Warrant: a standard applied to one group and no other is a pretext, not a principle.
      Evidence: —
  L3  And the empirics cut the other way → young voters form independent views
      Warrant: in Austria and Scotland, young voters saw the information and split from their parents more often than Opp assumes.
      Evidence: [[examples-bank#Austria — voting age 16]] & [[examples-bank#Scotland — 2014 independence referendum]] — young first-timers diverged from parents and voted seriously

IMPACT: defeats the strongest Opp line (parental influence) on both principle and evidence.
  Weighing: consistency — the symmetry argument means Opp must either disenfranchise adults too or drop the test.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: 16s live at home so parental pressure is stronger than for adults.  → I say: stronger proximity, but the data still shows independent divergence — and proximity-based pressure would also bar students, spouses; we don't.
  They say: adult "influence" is chosen; a minor's isn't.  → I say: the ballot is secret for both; if a secret ballot protects a swayed adult's real choice, it protects a swayed 16-year-old's just the same.
