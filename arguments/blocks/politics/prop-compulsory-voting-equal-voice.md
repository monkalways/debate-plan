ARGUMENT: compulsory turnout forces politicians to serve the poor, not just the rich who show up
Side: Prop        Motion type: Policy
Burden it serves: proves compulsory voting produces fairer policy for the marginalized — a welfare + fairness gain, not just legitimacy

CLAIM: Compulsory voting forces politicians to serve everyone — not just the wealthy and old who already turn out — because when everyone is counted, ignoring the poor costs seats.

CHAIN:
  L1  voluntary voting → turnout skews rich / old / educated
      Warrant: the poor and young face higher barriers (can't take the day off work, harder to register) and lower efficacy (they've learned the system ignores them), so they vote less.
  L2  skewed turnout → politicians serve whoever shows up
      Warrant: a politician's first goal is re-election, so platforms chase the reliable voters (pensioners, property-owners) and quietly ignore non-voters.
  L3  compulsory voting (+ election-day holiday) → everyone counted → politicians must court the poor
      Warrant: once the poor vote too, neglecting them costs seats; the model also removes the barrier directly by making election day a holiday.

IMPACT: policy shifts toward the whole population — the people hit hardest by bad policy finally carry electoral weight → fairer outcomes for millions of the most vulnerable.
  Weighing: scope / stakeholder-priority — a few comfortable people mildly inconvenienced at being made to show up, versus genuine political voice for millions of the most vulnerable. It isn't close.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: non-voters simply choose to stay home — it's their free choice, forcing them changes nothing.  → I say: for the poor it isn't a free choice, it's a *barrier* (time off work, registration, learned futility); compulsion + a voting holiday removes both the excuse and the barrier. [[mechanism-bank#Vitiated Consent / Manufactured Choice]]
  They say: compulsory voting won't change outcomes — every non-voting Liberal is offset by a non-voting Conservative (a wash).  → I say: turnout isn't ideologically neutral — anti-government voters abstain more, so compulsion moves the *median* voter, not just the count.
