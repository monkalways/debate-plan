ARGUMENT: first vote at 16 forms a durable habit that raises lifelong turnout
Side: Prop        Motion type: Policy
Burden it serves: proves the reform raises turnout permanently, strengthening democratic legitimacy — an even-if win independent of the maturity clash

CLAIM: Moving the first vote to 16 builds a voting habit that sticks for life, because it lands in a stable environment instead of the most unstable year.

CHAIN:
  L1  First vote at 18 → it often misfires
      Warrant: 18 is the single most unstable year — moving out, starting university/a job at once — so the first ballot is a bad experience or never happens.
      Evidence: —
  L2  First vote at 16 → it succeeds
      Warrant: a 16-year-old votes from a stable, structured base (family, school, time and support), so the first vote is easy, familiar and routine.
      Evidence: [[examples-bank#Scotland — 2014 independence referendum]] — ~three-quarters of 16–17s turned out vs ~half of 18–24s
  L3  A successful first vote → lifelong turnout
      Warrant: voting is habit-forming; a habit that starts, keeps going — carried forward and passed to their own children.

IMPACT: permanently higher turnout → a more legitimate democracy (a system that hears more of the governed).
  Weighing: timeframe/durability — "Opposition gambles the habit on the worst possible year to start; ours starts when it's most likely to stick." Even-if: even if Opp wins maturity, higher genuine turnout still carries the round.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: turnout rises now but fades — it's a novelty bump.  → I say: the mechanism is habit-formation, not novelty; Scotland's first-timers out-turned the 18–24 cohort precisely because the stable setting groove-set the habit.
  They say: 16-year-olds only turn out because parents drag them.  → I say: that stable scaffold is the point of the habit mechanism, and Austria shows their choices diverged from parents — real participation being habituated, not proxy voting.
