ARGUMENT: the franchise is about whose choice counts, not who passes a knowledge test
Side: Prop        Motion type: Policy   [recurring rebuttal move]
Burden it serves: neutralises the whole "they're too immature/inexperienced/uninformed" family of Opp attacks by rejecting its premise

CLAIM: Voting was never a competency contest, so "16-year-olds aren't informed enough" isn't an argument for exclusion.

CHAIN:
  L1  Democracy = rule by the people → not rule by the qualified
      Warrant: the point of a vote is to register a choice, not to certify knowledge; the moment you gate it on competence you've stopped being a democracy.
      Evidence: —
  L2  We already refuse the competence test for adults → applying it to 16s is inventing a new bar
      Warrant: we don't test 40-year-olds for knowledge before they vote; plenty of adults "vote on vibes, on family, on habit," yet no one strips their ballot.
      Evidence: —
  L3  No competence test for anyone → excluding 16s on competence is arbitrary
      Warrant: a standard applied to one group and no other isn't a principle, it's a pretext.

IMPACT: collapses the maturity/inexperience case — Opp's central line — before it does work.
  Weighing: consistency/principle — a test we refuse to apply to any adult cannot justify excluding one group of citizens.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: there's a difference — 16s are objectively less informed on average.  → I say: so are many adults, and we don't disenfranchise the least-informed 40-year-old; average competence has never been the franchise test.
  They say: you can't gain experience without a first vote anyway.  → I say: exactly — "you don't learn to vote by not voting; Opposition's cure for inexperience is to guarantee more of it." (competence is acquired by doing, so exclusion guarantees the inexperience it fears)
