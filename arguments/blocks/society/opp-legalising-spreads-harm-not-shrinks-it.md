ARGUMENT: legalising doesn't shrink the harm, it universalises it (harm-reduction backfire)  🔁
Side: Opp        Motion type: Policy (Legalise-and-regulate)
Burden it serves: the rebuttal SHIELD to Prop's best line — "regulation is safer than the black market." Concede the black market is real, then turn the safety frame: legalising moves harm from a risky FEW to a coerced EVERYONE.

CLAIM: "Regulate it and it's safer" measures the wrong thing — legalising doesn't reduce the per-user danger, it multiplies the number of users and drives doses up, so total harm rises.

CHAIN:
  L1  Legalise → the whole field enters, not just today's shadow users
      Warrant: what was a hidden minority becomes the compulsory norm (see the coercion block) — you don't shrink the pool, you flood it.
      Evidence: —
  L2  Everyone in → doses climb (the arms race)
      Warrant: to stay ahead of now-legal rivals, users escalate; regulation sets a floor of access, not a ceiling on ambition.
      Evidence: —
  L3  Norm cascades to juniors/amateurs
      Warrant: legal use at the top removes the deterrent (no career-ending ban), so those below copy the pros — the harm reaches the most vulnerable bodies.
      Evidence: [[examples-bank#Adolescent steroids — stunted growth]]

IMPACT: total harm INCREASES — more people, higher doses, and the harm reaches developing juniors it never touched before. "Safer for the doper" is bought by harming everyone else.
  Weighing: scope × magnitude — concede regulation may cut risk for the existing few; that's dwarfed by universalised participation + escalating doses + the junior cascade. Spreading a harm to protect its current victims is a bad trade.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: regulation + medical supervision is safer than dangerous shadow use.  → I say: concede shadow use is real — then turn it: you're not shrinking the harm, you're spreading it. Safer-per-user × far-more-users × higher-doses = more total damage, now including kids.
  They say: banning is what CREATES the black market.  → I say: the ban confines harm to a deterred minority; lifting it doesn't clean the harm up, it makes it the entry price for everyone who wants to compete.
