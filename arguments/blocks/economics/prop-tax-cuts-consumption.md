ARGUMENT: price + supply + norms make the tax actually reduce sugary-drink consumption
Side: Prop        Motion type: Policy
Burden it serves: proves the mechanism works at all — the tax changes behaviour, not just prices

CLAIM: A sugary-drink tax measurably lowers consumption through three reinforcing channels (price, supply, norms).

CHAIN:
  L1  Tax → relative price of the healthy option falls → consumer switches at point of purchase
      Warrant: when a buyer is torn between two near-substitutes (water vs. Coke), price is the deciding factor — a small swing flips the marginal purchase.
      Evidence: [[examples-bank#Tobacco taxes & plain packaging]] (price moves behaviour even on somewhat-inelastic goods); [[examples-bank#Mexico sugar-sweetened beverage tax]]
  L2  Consumers switch → producers shift toward healthier lines
      Warrant: falling demand cuts profit on sugary drinks; firms chase higher-demand products, so supply follows demand and healthy options get cheaper.
      Evidence: —
  L3  Lower consumption + cheaper healthy supply → social norm shifts → consumption falls further
      Warrant: visible behaviour change makes the healthy choice the default and strips status from the unhealthy one, compounding the price effect.

IMPACT: population-wide fall in sugar intake → less obesity over time; scope = hundreds of thousands of marginal consumers moved by a small price change.
  Weighing: scope — a small price change reaches every low-margin buyer at once, which beats the opp's individual-liberty cost because the liberty lost (a few cents on one drink) is trivial against a population-scale health gain.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: demand for soda is inelastic, a tax just takes money without changing behaviour.  → I say: tobacco is addictive and taxes still cut smoking; soda has cheap real substitutes (water/juice), so the switch is easier, not harder.
  They say: the three channels loop back on each other, you never land an impact.  → I say: state the terminal impact explicitly — fewer obese people over a decade — don't just restate the market cycle.
