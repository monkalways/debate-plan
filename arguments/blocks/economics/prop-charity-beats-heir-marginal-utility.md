ARGUMENT: an heir's dollar buys less welfare than a charity's dollar (diminishing marginal utility)
Side: Prop        Motion type: Abolition
Burden it serves: society-wide welfare gain (utilitarian).

CLAIM: Money that would have padded a comfortable heir does far more good redirected to people in need — "more bang for your buck."

CHAIN:
  L1  Abolish inheritance → wealth does not pass to the heir
      Warrant: transfer is banned, so the money must go somewhere else (charity / spent-in-life / state).
      Evidence: — [THE FLAW: the routing is asserted, never explained]
  L2  Redirected wealth → reaches lower-baseline recipients (the needy)
      Warrant: charitable giving or incentive channels it to people with unmet basic needs.
  L3  Lower-baseline recipient → more welfare per dollar
      Warrant: diminishing marginal utility — a dollar feeds a starving child rather than adding a marginal luxury for a comfortable heir.

IMPACT: greater aggregate welfare from the same wealth. Value: total societal utility.
  Weighing: magnitude / marginal-utility — feeding the needy outweighs enabling a grandchild's leisure. (The "bang for your buck" weigh — the speech's single best comparative in two days.)

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: abolishing inheritance does not route money to charity — it gets spent, hidden, or seized by the state, not given to the poor.  → I say: specify the mechanism — a charitable-giving incentive, or "spent in life" stimulating the economy, or state redistribution. Without it, L1 is unproven.
  They say: heirs from poor families need that dollar as much as any charity recipient.  → I say: means-test — this bites hardest on large estates and barely touches modest ones.
