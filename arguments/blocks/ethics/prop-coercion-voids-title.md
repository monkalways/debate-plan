ARGUMENT: goods taken by force or colonial coercion were never owned — time doesn't launder theft
Side: Prop        Motion type: Value/Policy   (restitution family — transfers to reparations / repatriation / "return X" motions)
Burden it serves: establishes that the origin nation is the RIGHTFUL owner, so "return" is just restoring stolen property — the root justice question the round turns on.

CLAIM: An object taken by force or under colonial coercion was never legitimately transferred, so the taker never acquired real title — and the passage of time doesn't cure the theft.

CHAIN:
  L1  Object taken by force / colonial coercion → the "sale" or transfer is void
      Warrant: a coerced party can't give valid consent; you can't sign away what you were forced to hand over, so no ownership ever passed. "Coercion means no deal."
      Evidence: [[examples-bank#Benin Bronzes]] — looted in the 1897 Punitive Expedition (spoils of war, not a sale).
  L2  No valid transfer → the taker holds custody, not title
      Warrant: possessing stolen goods isn't the same as owning them; the original owner's claim never extinguished.
  L3  Original claim survives → time doesn't launder it → return is owed
      Warrant: a wrong doesn't become right by ageing; if it did, every thief would just have to wait. Justice restores the rightful owner.

IMPACT: the origin community is the true owner; keeping the object is ongoing possession of stolen property, and return is the minimum justice requires.
  Weighing: stakeholder-priority — the rightful owner's claim outranks the current holder's convenience, because a title built on force isn't a title at all.

THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: Britain acquired these lawfully and holds clear legal title.  → I say: legal title downstream of a coerced taking is void at the root — the original seller had no right to transfer, so nothing valid passed; a clean paper trail on a stolen object is still a stolen object.
  They say: you can't unpick every acquisition centuries later without emptying every museum.  → I say: the test isn't age, it's how it was taken — documented loot (a punitive raid) is exactly the clear case; "we can't fix everything" is no reason to fix nothing.
