ARGUMENT: caring for an object well doesn't cure the theft — ownership decides preservation  🔁
Side: Prop        Motion type: Value/Policy   (weighing / subsume principle — for any "but we look after it better" clash: restitution, reparations, adverse-possession-style motions)
Burden it serves: makes the justice question (who owns it) DECIDE the preservation question, so opp's "we preserve it better" can't win even if true.

CLAIM: Once we've shown the object was wrongfully taken, "but we look after it better" is just stewardship of stolen goods — you don't get to keep what you took by pointing at how nicely you keep it.

CHAIN:
  L1  Concede preservation at full strength → a looted object safely displayed is STILL looted
      Warrant: good care changes the object's condition, not its ownership; the theft is untouched by the polish.
  L2  So preservation is a fact about CARE, not about TITLE
      Warrant: how well you keep a thing says nothing about whether it's yours — a careful thief is still a thief.
  L3  Ownership therefore decides preservation, not the reverse → return still owed
      Warrant: the rightful owner is entitled to decide how their property is cared for (and can fund/loan for safekeeping); custody follows title, title doesn't follow custody.

IMPACT: opp's strongest practical line (we preserve it better) is subsumed — it can be entirely true and still lose, because it never answers whose object it is.
  Weighing: stakeholder-priority collapses the clash — the owner's claim decides who gets to preserve it, so Q1 (ownership) disposes of Q2 (preservation). "You don't get to keep what you took by showing how nicely you keep it."
  🔁 RE-ILLUSTRATION (scripted, day-20): *"If I steal a diamond chain from my friend but shine it every single day, that doesn't mean I'm allowed to keep it — it's still robbery."* A legal reply-move (re-dramatises a point already in the round, zero new matter); Yoodli-rated his sharpest line. THEY SAY → I SAY:
  They say: we have the money, expertise and stability to protect it; they don't.  → I say: that's an argument for offering conservation support or a loan-back, not for keeping it — caring for stolen goods well is still stewardship of stolen goods.
  They say: if it goes back it could be damaged, looted, or lost in conflict.  → I say: real risk, and the owner can choose to leave it in a secure museum — but risk of damage is a reason to help them protect it, never a reason to keep what isn't yours.
