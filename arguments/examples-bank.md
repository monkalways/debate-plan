# Examples Bank

Real-world examples, cases, and stats — tagged by theme. This bank directly attacks
the **Knowledge weakness (5/10)**: a debater who names a concrete example beats one who
stays abstract. One vivid, accurate example per argument is worth three assertions.

> ⚠️ **Verify before you cite.** Numbers below are directionally correct and stable as
> of training, but Lucas should **confirm the current figure** before using it in a
> round — judges punish a stat that's visibly out of date. Round numbers are fine
> ("over a billion", "roughly half"); false precision is dangerous.

> **How to use:** each entry has the **fact**, the **stat/case**, and **"use it for"**
> (which arguments it powers). Tag format: `theme · sub-theme · which-side`.

> 🔎 Entries marked **✅ Verified** with a *Powers [[block]]* line were **distilled from Lucas's
> own graded speeches (Days 1–11)** and web-verified before banking — the block shows where the
> stat fired. Several **corrected a bad number he used on air** (flagged in the entry).

# Economics & Development  *(Week 1 domain)*

## Inequality & mobility

**Global wealth concentration** · inequality · prop-on-redistribution
- The richest **10%** own roughly **three-quarters** of global wealth; the bottom 50% own about **2%**. *(The top 1% alone own a contested ~38–45% — use the 10% figure, it's far safer to defend.)*
- *Use it for:* the Inequality/Compounding-Advantage mechanism — evidence that wealth concentrates without intervention.

**The "Great Gatsby Curve"** · mobility · prop-on-redistribution
- Across countries, higher inequality correlates with *lower* social mobility — unequal societies tend to be less mobile, not more.
- *Use it for:* rebutting "inequality is fine if there's mobility" — it shows the two move together.
- *Weakest link:* it's a **correlation, not proof of cause**. Don't lean on it alone — pair it with the compounding-advantage *mechanism* so you're explaining *why*, not just pointing at a trend.

**Scandinavian model** · redistribution · prop
- High-tax, high-redistribution states (Denmark, Sweden) combine strong welfare with competitive economies and high happiness rankings.
- *Use it for:* redistribution doesn't have to kill growth (defends the inequality mechanism's weakest link).

## Markets, regulation & failure

**2008 financial crisis** · deregulation · prop-on-regulation
- Under-regulated mortgage and derivatives markets triggered a global crash; millions lost homes and jobs.
- *Use it for:* market-failure mechanism — markets left alone can self-destruct; regulation prevents systemic risk.

**Acid rain & cap-and-trade (US, 1990s)** · externality · prop-on-regulation
- A market-based cap on sulphur emissions cut acid rain sharply and faster/cheaper than predicted.
- *Use it for:* pricing an externality *works* — the strongest real proof for carbon-tax / market-failure cases.

**Tobacco taxes & plain packaging** · incentive distortion · prop
- Higher tobacco taxes and plain packaging measurably reduced smoking rates in Australia and the UK.
- *Use it for:* the incentive-distortion mechanism — price/friction changes behavior at scale. (Note: tobacco demand is *somewhat* inelastic, so this also shows taxes can work even on addictive goods — useful against the elasticity attack.)

## Trade, development & aid

**China's poverty reduction** · growth/development · opp-on-aid, prop-on-growth
- Roughly 800 million people lifted out of extreme poverty over ~40 years, driven mainly by growth, industrialisation, and trade.
- ✅ **Anchor stat — safe to cite** as "around 800 million." Robust across sources; say it with full confidence.
- *Use it for:* "growth, not aid, drives development" — and the development-vs-environment clash.

**South Korea vs. aid-dependence** · development · prop-on-growth
- Went from aid recipient to advanced economy in a generation via export-led industrial policy.
- *Use it for:* state-guided development *can* work (defends industrial-policy / public-goods arguments).

**Extreme poverty decline** · development · context
- The share of the world in extreme poverty fell from ~36% (1990) to under ~10% — one of the great underreported facts.
- *Use it for:* framing/characterisation on any development motion; rebuts "globalisation only helps the rich."

## Automation & labour

**Automation & jobs** · technology/labour · both sides
- Studies suggest a large share of current tasks are automatable, but history (ATMs → more bank branches) shows technology often *shifts* rather than destroys jobs.
- *Use it for:* both the "automation harms workers" chain *and* its rebuttal — know both directions.

## Sin taxes, welfare & public health  *(distilled, Days 1 & 7)*

**Mexico sugar-sweetened beverage tax (2014)** · sin-tax / incentive · prop-on-soda-tax
- A ~1-peso/litre (~10%) tax cut taxed-drink sales ~12% in year one and ~7.6% on average over two years; the **lowest-income households cut purchases the most**. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* incentive-distortion + norm-cascade, and the **regressive-tax turn** (proof the poor respond most). Powers [[prop-tax-cuts-consumption]], [[prop-regressive-tax-turn]].
- *Weakest link:* measures purchases/sales, not obesity outcomes — pair with the mechanism, don't claim it "cured obesity."

**Obesity and depression link** *(CORRECTED — replaces the speech's bogus "350×")* · mental-health · prop
- Adults with obesity ≈ **55% higher lifetime depression risk**; adolescents ≈ **2× MDD**; relationship is **bidirectional**. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* the mental-health chain, [[prop-tax-improves-mental-health]] — an honest, defensible number.
- *Weakest link:* correlation and bidirectional — say "linked to," don't assert soda → depression one-way. **The transcript's "350× more likely" is FALSE — never cite it.**

**Canada poverty rate (StatCan MBM)** · poverty / welfare · opp-on-targeting
- Official poverty rate ≈ **7.4% (2021), ~9.9% (2022)** — Statistics Canada, Market Basket Measure. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* the fixed-pool/targeting mechanism ("most people aren't poor, so a universal payout mostly misses them"), [[opp-targeted-welfare-fixed-pool]].
- *Weakest link:* ⚠️ cite as "**roughly 7–10%, verify the year**," name the country (US is ~11–12% on a different measure), and do NOT convert to "93% don't need help" (non-sequitur).

**Finland basic income experiment (2017–18)** · UBI / welfare · both sides
- 2,000 unemployed Finns paid **€560/month** unconditionally for 2 years. Result: **no significant negative employment effect** (recipients worked slightly *more*, ~+6 days/yr) and **improved wellbeing/mental health**. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* the CORRECT read — it does NOT support "UBI makes people lazy." Honest Opp use: "wellbeing rose but the jobs boom never came, and it was costly to scale." Powers [[opp-cash-transfer-work-disincentive-dilemma]] (as a *cut* — the logical fork needs no stat).
- *Weakest link:* small sample, only *unemployed* recipients. **⚠️ Lucas's Day-7 claim that it reduced willingness to work is FABRICATED/REVERSED — drop it.**

## Key facts to memorise for this domain
- Extreme poverty: ~36% (1990) → <~10% today.
- Top 10% hold ~¾ of global wealth; bottom 50% hold a low single-digit %. *(Top 1% ~38–45% — contested; don't lead with it.)*
- China: ~800M lifted from extreme poverty in ~40 years.
- Cap-and-trade (US acid rain) and tobacco taxes are the two cleanest "pricing works" cases.
- 2008 = the go-to "deregulation causes systemic harm" case.
- Efficiency–equity tradeoff is the core clash: redistribution *can* cost growth (Opp) **or** Scandinavia shows it needn't (Prop).
- **Mexico soda tax** = sin-taxes change behaviour *and* the poor respond most; **Finland UBI** = no laziness effect (don't claim otherwise); **Canada ~7–10% poverty** = most people aren't poor (targeting argument).

# International Relations & Security  *(Week 2 domain)*

## Deterrence & nuclear

**The "long peace" / nuclear deterrence** · deterrence · prop-on-keeping-deterrents
- No direct war between great powers since 1945 — many credit Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD): no one starts a war they can't survive.
- *Use it for:* the deterrence mechanism — arsenals *prevent* war by making it unwinnable.
- *Weakest link:* it's **correlation, not proof** — and it only takes one failure to be catastrophic. Pair with the security-dilemma mechanism if you're on Opp.

**Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)** · deterrence · both sides
- The closest the world came to nuclear war; defused by back-channel diplomacy, not force.
- *Use it for:* Prop-on-diplomacy (talking beats posturing) **or** Opp-on-deterrence (it nearly failed — deterrence is luck-dependent). Know both directions.

**Ukraine & the Budapest Memorandum (1994)** · proliferation · context
- Ukraine gave up the nuclear weapons on its soil for written security "assurances"; it was invaded anyway (2014, 2022).
- *Use it for:* why states *want* nuclear weapons (paper guarantees don't deter); the limits of security assurances. Keep it factual — it's a live conflict.

## Alliances & collective security

**NATO Article 5** · alliances · prop-on-alliances
- Collective defence — an attack on one is an attack on all. Invoked **only once**, after 9/11 (2001). Finland and Sweden joined after Russia's 2022 invasion.
- *Use it for:* alliances deter aggression and expand *in response to* threat.
- *Weakest link:* Opp's "security dilemma" — expansion can look offensive to rivals and provoke the very arms race it fears.

**Pre-WWI arms race** · security dilemma · opp-on-militarisation
- The Anglo-German naval build-up before 1914: each side's "defensive" arming looked offensive to the other, spiralling toward war.
- *Use it for:* the security-dilemma mechanism — buying "security" can *reduce* it.

## Intervention & humanitarian action

**Rwanda genocide (1994)** · intervention · prop-on-R2P
- The world stood by; an estimated **800,000+** people were killed in ~100 days.
- *Use it for:* the cost of *inaction* — the strongest case for humanitarian intervention / Responsibility to Protect.

**Libya (2011)** · intervention · opp-on-intervention
- A NATO intervention (justified on R2P) toppled Gaddafi but left years of chaos and civil war.
- *Use it for:* even well-intentioned intervention can worsen outcomes — the natural pair/counter to Rwanda. Holding *both* lets you win either side.

**Iraq War (2003)** · legitimacy · opp-on-intervention
- Launched on faulty WMD intelligence, without a clear UN mandate; long insurgency and lost legitimacy followed.
- *Use it for:* why **multilateral legitimacy** (a UN mandate) matters, not just capability.

**Marshall Plan (1948)** · reconstruction/soft power · prop-on-aid
- US aid rebuilt Western Europe after WWII and stabilised its democracies.
- *Use it for:* rebuilding/soft power beats punishment; investment buys stability and alliances.

## Sanctions & economic statecraft

**Apartheid sanctions (South Africa)** · sanctions · prop-on-sanctions
- Broad international sanctions and divestment added pressure that helped end apartheid.
- *Use it for:* sanctions *can* work — when they're broad, legitimate, and multilateral.
- *Weakest link:* how decisive they were is contested; don't claim they *alone* ended it.

**Iraq sanctions (1990s)** · sanctions · opp-on-sanctions
- Sweeping sanctions are widely held to have harmed ordinary civilians more than the regime.
- *Use it for:* the Opp turn — blunt sanctions punish the powerless while elites adapt. (Pairs with South Africa for either side.)

---

## Key facts to memorise for this domain
- **1945** = no great-power war since (the "long peace"); MAD is the claimed mechanism — but it's correlation.
- **Article 5 invoked once**, after 9/11; NATO *grew* after 2022 (Finland, Sweden).
- **Rwanda (~800k, 100 days)** = the go-to "cost of inaction" case; **Libya/Iraq** = the "intervention backfires / needs legitimacy" counter.
- **Marshall Plan** = rebuild-not-punish; **Budapest Memorandum** = why paper guarantees fail.
- Core clash: **sovereignty vs. human protection** (can outsiders intervene?) and **deterrence vs. security-dilemma** (does arming make us safer or spiral us to war?).

# Science, Tech & Environment  *(Week 3 domain)*

## Climate & energy

**Montreal Protocol (1987)** · environmental cooperation · prop-on-treaties
- The world phased out ozone-destroying CFCs; the ozone layer is now healing — the great success story of environmental treaties.
- ✅ **Anchor case — safe to cite with full confidence.** The cleanest proof that coordinated global action *works*.
- *Use it for:* Prop on any "international action can solve this" motion; contrast it with why climate is harder (diffuse, costly, no easy substitute).

**Paris Agreement (2015)** · climate coordination · both sides
- ~196 parties agreed to limit warming "well below 2°C," aiming for 1.5°C — but targets are **non-binding**.
- *Use it for:* Prop (global coordination exists) **or** Opp (voluntary pledges without enforcement underdeliver).

**Renewable cost collapse** · energy transition · prop-on-green
- Solar power costs fell **~90%** over the 2010s; new solar and wind are now often the *cheapest* source of new electricity.
- *Use it for:* the green transition is economically rational, not just moral — defeats "we can't afford it."
- ⚠️ **Verify the current figure** before citing; say "roughly 90% cheaper" not a false-precise number.

**Germany's nuclear phase-out (Energiewende)** · energy tradeoffs · opp-on-nuclear-exit
- Germany grew renewables but shut its nuclear plants, leaning harder on coal/gas in the short term.
- *Use it for:* energy transitions involve real tradeoffs; the nuclear debate (low-carbon but politically toxic).

**Emissions inequality (giant vs. poor nation)** · climate-justice · prop-on-development / opp-on-uniform-climate  *(distilled, Day 6)*
- The US emits **~an eighth of global CO2** (~11–13% CO2-only; ~10% all-GHG); a poor country like **Nigeria emits well under 1%**. China is the largest single emitter (~29% GHG). ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* Negligible-Contributor / Ladder-Pulling — a poor nation greening itself barely moves the total. Powers [[prop-poor-nations-emit-negligibly]].
- *Weakest link:* developing-world emissions are fastest-growing and per-capita flips the fairness picture; **cite the ratio ("well under 1% vs ~an eighth"), not the decimals**, and pin the denominator (CO2 vs all-GHG).

## AI & automation

**Algorithmic bias** · AI harms · prop-on-AI-regulation
- Studies (NIST, MIT) found facial-recognition systems markedly less accurate on darker-skinned faces and women.
- *Use it for:* AI is not neutral — it can encode and scale discrimination, so it needs oversight.

**Deepfakes & misinformation** · trust/tech · prop-on-regulation
- AI-generated fake audio/video/images threaten elections and public trust; hard to detect at scale.
- *Use it for:* the regulation-lag mechanism — capability is outpacing our safeguards. (Cross-links to the Media domain.)

**Automation shifts, not just destroys, jobs** · labour/tech · both sides
- Cross-reference the **ATMs → more bank branches** case in the Economics domain — technology often *reshapes* work rather than erasing it.
- *Use it for:* rebutting "AI will end all jobs" — but concede the *transition* harms specific workers.

## Surveillance & privacy

**Snowden revelations (2013)** · surveillance · opp-on-mass-surveillance
- Leaks exposed mass government surveillance of ordinary citizens, igniting the privacy-vs-security debate.
- *Use it for:* security powers expand quietly and overreach without oversight.

**GDPR (EU, 2018)** · data protection · prop-on-regulation
- A strong EU data-protection law that became a *de facto* global standard (the "Brussels effect").
- *Use it for:* regulation *can* protect people and set worldwide norms — governments aren't powerless against big tech.

## Biotech & health

**mRNA COVID-19 vaccines (~2020)** · science funding / animal research · prop-on-innovation, opp-on-animal-research
- Safe, effective vaccines were developed in roughly a **year** — the fastest in history — on the back of years of public research funding.
- **Animal-testing angle (distilled, Day 11):** every widely used COVID vaccine (Pfizer, Moderna, Oxford/AZ) was **tested in animals — mice, hamsters, rhesus macaques** — before/alongside human trials. ✅ Verified.
- ✅ **Strong, safe case.** *Use it for:* what coordinated science funding + urgency can achieve (innovation as a public good); AND research-saves-lives / safety-testing — even the fastest vaccine went through the animal pipeline. Powers [[opp-research-saves-lives]].
- *Weakest link:* on the animal angle, PCRM/PETA contest whether the tests were strictly *necessary* — say "safety-tested in animals," not "impossible without."

**Smallpox eradication (1980)** · global health · prop-on-cooperation
- The only human disease ever eradicated, via a coordinated global vaccination campaign.
- *Use it for:* global cooperation delivers what no single state could — a clean companion to the Montreal Protocol.

**CRISPR & the 2018 gene-edited babies** · bioethics · opp-on-enhancement
- He Jiankui created the first gene-edited babies in 2018 — met with near-universal scientific condemnation.
- *Use it for:* bioethics / "designer babies" / where to draw the line on enhancement; why some science needs hard limits.

**Antibiotic resistance** · commons/health · context
- Overuse of antibiotics is breeding resistant bacteria — a slow-moving global health threat.
- *Use it for:* a tragedy-of-the-commons in health; why individually rational use is collectively disastrous.

- *Use it for:* research-saves-lives — a landmark life-saving drug that exists only because of animal research. Powers [[opp-research-saves-lives]].

- *Weakest link:* it's PETA's own estimate — use it AS a turn ("even PETA admits…"), don't present it as a neutral audited figure.

**Gallup 2015 animal-rights poll** *(replaces the bogus "90%")* · public opinion · opp

- *Weakest link:* the 32% wanting EQUAL rights is a sizeable, RISING minority — frame as "a clear majority accept using animals," don't overclaim consensus. **The transcript's "90% believe animal life worth less" has no source — use Gallup instead.**

# Politics & Governance  *(Week 4 domain — distilled Days 4–5, 12)*

## Franchise & turnout

**Austria — voting age 16 (2007)** · franchise / youth-turnout · prop-on-lowering-voting-age
- First EU country to lower the federal voting age to 16. 16–17s turn out at **higher rates than older first-time voters (18–20)** (~+2 to +10 pts) and near the electorate average; their vote *choices* are **as considered** as older voters'. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* enfranchisement + habit-formation; rebutting immaturity/parental-influence. Powers [[prop-16yo-are-stakeholders]], [[prop-first-vote-builds-lifelong-habit]], [[prop-dont-apply-a-test-we-spare-adults]].
- *Weakest link:* say "higher than older *first-time* voters," NOT "beats everyone in their twenties"; 16–17s show somewhat lower general political interest — the load-bearing finding is turnout + choice-quality.

**Scotland — 2014 independence referendum (votes at 16)** · franchise / youth-turnout · prop
- 16–17s enfranchised for the first time; an **estimated ~75% turned out vs ~54% of 18–24s** (+~21 pts). ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* habit-formation ("first vote in a stable setting sticks"), [[prop-first-vote-builds-lifelong-habit]].
- *Weakest link:* survey estimate, and a HIGH-salience one-off referendum; the home/family effect partly explains it — concede the scaffold, then argue that *is* the habit mechanism. Say "an estimated ~three-quarters vs ~half."

## Compulsory voting  *(distilled, Day 12)*

**Australia — compulsory voting (since 1924)** · turnout / compulsory-voting · prop
- Compulsory since the 1924 amendment; turnout jumped from **58.7% (1922) → 91.4% (1925)** and has stayed **~90%+** since. The **informal (spoiled/invalid) ballot rate is ~5%** (2025 House of Reps ≈5.5%); true "donkey votes" (numbered straight down the page) are only ~**1–2%**. ✅ Verified (AEC).
- *Use it for:* compulsory-voting mandate + equal-voice; the "~5% is a small honest cost" shield. Powers [[prop-compulsory-voting-mandate]], [[prop-compulsory-voting-equal-voice]], [[prop-informal-votes-wash-both-worlds]].
- *Weakest link:* say "under 60% to over 90%" (NOT "50%→90%"), and call ~5% the **informal** rate, NOT "donkey votes" — a sharp opp catches the mislabel, and a raw informal rate is arguably *their* evidence unless you frame it as the small honest cost.

**Canada — 2025 federal turnout** · turnout · prop-on-compulsory-voting (status-quo baseline)
- The 45th general election (Apr 28, 2025) drew **69.0%** turnout of registered electors — highest since 1993 — so **~31% didn't vote**. ✅ Verified (Elections Canada).
- *Use it for:* the status-quo "minority mandate" problem a compulsory-voting Prop fixes — the third of the electorate that stayed home could have changed the result. Powers [[prop-compulsory-voting-mandate]].
- *Weakest link:* it's turnout of *registered* electors (voting-age-population turnout is a bit lower); 69% is actually *high* for a voluntary system — frame it "even our best case leaves a third silent," not "turnout is collapsing."

## Key facts to memorise for this domain
- **Austria (votes at 16 since 2007)** + **Scotland (2014 referendum, ~75% of 16–17s voted)** = the twin "16-year-olds turn out AND vote seriously" cases — the backbone of any lower-the-voting-age case.
- **Australia (compulsory since 1924, ~90%+ turnout, ~5% informal)** + **Canada (2025 ≈ 69% turnout, ~31% absent)** = the compulsory-vs-voluntary pair — the backbone of any compulsory-voting case. Call ~5% the *informal* rate, not "donkey votes".

## Sport, doping & the body  *(distilled, Day 14)*

**Anabolic steroids — long-term harm** · sport / doping / health · opp-on-PEDs
- Long-term high-dose anabolic-androgenic steroid use is linked to serious, partly **irreversible** harm: cardiovascular damage (cardiomyopathy — an enlarged, weakened heart — atherosclerosis, hypertension, raised sudden-cardiac-death risk) and endocrine disruption (the body's own testosterone/fertility system stays suppressed). Some heart changes persist after stopping. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* the health-harm limb of any PED/doping motion, or any "the regulated version is still dangerous" turn. Powers [[opp-legalising-spreads-harm-not-shrinks-it]].
- *Weakest link:* state it as a *general, documented, partly-irreversible* harm — **NO invented percentages** (zero-fabrication rule). "Serious, documented, doesn't fully reverse" is enough.

**Adolescent steroids — stunted growth** · sport / doping / youth-harm · opp-on-PEDs
- In teenagers whose bones are still growing, anabolic steroids can trigger **premature closure of the growth plates (epiphyseal plates)** — once closed, bones can't lengthen, so the person ends up **permanently shorter** than their genetic potential. Irreversible. ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* the junior-cascade / "harm reaches the young" limb — the irreversible, non-consenting-victim impact adult-autonomy can't touch. Powers [[opp-elite-behaviour-cascades-to-youth]], [[opp-legalising-spreads-harm-not-shrinks-it]].
- *Weakest link:* Grade-8 phrasing — "in a still-growing teen these drugs slam the growth plates shut early, so the bones stop lengthening." No numbers needed.

# History, Culture & Restitution  *(distilled, Day 19)*

## Colonial looting & repatriation

**Benin Bronzes** · restitution / colonial looting · prop-on-repatriation
- Looted by British forces in the **1897 Punitive Expedition** — Benin City burned, the royal palace stripped, thousands of bronzes and ivories taken as "spoils of war" (not purchased). Now scattered across **100+ museums**. Returns have begun: **Germany transferred ownership of 1,000+ bronzes to Nigeria (2022)** — the largest repatriation of African art in history — and London's **Horniman Museum agreed to return 72 (2022)**; the **British Museum has returned none** (blocked by the 1963 British Museum Act). ✅ Verified.
- *Use it for:* the illegitimate-title / restitution mechanism — a *documented* looting (not a murky purchase), the clean case that beats "you can't unpick every acquisition." Powers [[prop-coercion-voids-title]], [[prop-stewardship-of-stolen-goods]].
- *Weakest link:* say "**looted in an 1897 military raid**" (rock-solid) + "**some institutions have started returning them**"; do NOT claim the British Museum returned them (it hasn't). Round numbers ("thousands of bronzes," "over a hundred museums").

**2025 Louvre heist** · museum security / concentration-risk · prop-on-dispersing-artifacts
