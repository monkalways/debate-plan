# Examples Bank

Real-world examples, cases, and stats — tagged by theme. This bank directly attacks
the **Knowledge weakness (5/10)**: a debater who names a concrete example beats one who
stays abstract. One vivid, accurate example per argument is worth three assertions.

> ⚠️ **Verify before you cite.** Numbers below are directionally correct and stable as
> of training, but Lucas should **confirm the current figure** before using it in a
> round — judges punish a stat that's visibly out of date. Round numbers are fine
> ("over a billion", "roughly half"); false precision is dangerous.

> **How to use:** each entry has the **fact**, the **stat/case**, and **"use it for"**
> (which arguments it powers). Tag format: `theme · sub-theme · which-side`.

# Economics & Development  *(Week 1 domain)*

## Inequality & mobility

**Global wealth concentration** · inequality · prop-on-redistribution
- The richest **10%** own roughly **three-quarters** of global wealth; the bottom 50% own about **2%**. *(The top 1% alone own a contested ~38–45% — use the 10% figure, it's far safer to defend.)*
- *Use it for:* the Inequality/Compounding-Advantage mechanism — evidence that wealth concentrates without intervention.

**The "Great Gatsby Curve"** · mobility · prop-on-redistribution
- Across countries, higher inequality correlates with *lower* social mobility — unequal societies tend to be less mobile, not more.
- *Use it for:* rebutting "inequality is fine if there's mobility" — it shows the two move together.
- *Weakest link:* it's a **correlation, not proof of cause**. Don't lean on it alone — pair it with the compounding-advantage *mechanism* so you're explaining *why*, not just pointing at a trend.

**Scandinavian model** · redistribution · prop
- High-tax, high-redistribution states (Denmark, Sweden) combine strong welfare with competitive economies and high happiness rankings.
- *Use it for:* redistribution doesn't have to kill growth (defends the inequality mechanism's weakest link).

## Markets, regulation & failure

**2008 financial crisis** · deregulation · prop-on-regulation
- Under-regulated mortgage and derivatives markets triggered a global crash; millions lost homes and jobs.
- *Use it for:* market-failure mechanism — markets left alone can self-destruct; regulation prevents systemic risk.

**Acid rain & cap-and-trade (US, 1990s)** · externality · prop-on-regulation
- A market-based cap on sulphur emissions cut acid rain sharply and faster/cheaper than predicted.
- *Use it for:* pricing an externality *works* — the strongest real proof for carbon-tax / market-failure cases.

**Tobacco taxes & plain packaging** · incentive distortion · prop
- Higher tobacco taxes and plain packaging measurably reduced smoking rates in Australia and the UK.
- *Use it for:* the incentive-distortion mechanism — price/friction changes behavior at scale. (Note: tobacco demand is *somewhat* inelastic, so this also shows taxes can work even on addictive goods — useful against the elasticity attack.)

## Trade, development & aid

**China's poverty reduction** · growth/development · opp-on-aid, prop-on-growth
- Roughly 800 million people lifted out of extreme poverty over ~40 years, driven mainly by growth, industrialisation, and trade.
- ✅ **Anchor stat — safe to cite** as "around 800 million." Robust across sources; say it with full confidence.
- *Use it for:* "growth, not aid, drives development" — and the development-vs-environment clash.

**South Korea vs. aid-dependence** · development · prop-on-growth
- Went from aid recipient to advanced economy in a generation via export-led industrial policy.
- *Use it for:* state-guided development *can* work (defends industrial-policy / public-goods arguments).

**Extreme poverty decline** · development · context
- The share of the world in extreme poverty fell from ~36% (1990) to under ~10% — one of the great underreported facts.
- *Use it for:* framing/characterisation on any development motion; rebuts "globalisation only helps the rich."

## Automation & labour

**Automation & jobs** · technology/labour · both sides
- Studies suggest a large share of current tasks are automatable, but history (ATMs → more bank branches) shows technology often *shifts* rather than destroys jobs.
- *Use it for:* both the "automation harms workers" chain *and* its rebuttal — know both directions.

## Key facts to memorise for this domain
- Extreme poverty: ~36% (1990) → <~10% today.
- Top 10% hold ~¾ of global wealth; bottom 50% hold a low single-digit %. *(Top 1% ~38–45% — contested; don't lead with it.)*
- China: ~800M lifted from extreme poverty in ~40 years.
- Cap-and-trade (US acid rain) and tobacco taxes are the two cleanest "pricing works" cases.
- 2008 = the go-to "deregulation causes systemic harm" case.
- Efficiency–equity tradeoff is the core clash: redistribution *can* cost growth (Opp) **or** Scandinavia shows it needn't (Prop).
