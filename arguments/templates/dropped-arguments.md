# Dropped Arguments — The 5-Step Reconstruction

**Use this when the opposition ignores one of your arguments.** A dropped argument is a concession — but it only wins you the round if you make the judge *see* that it was dropped, rebuild it cleanly, and weigh it. Don't just say "they dropped it"; walk these five steps in order.

---

## ① Flag the drop
> "Our [first / second] argument was that ______________. We introduced it in ______________'s speech. Across ______ minutes of opposition, the only thing you heard about it was ______________ [ / "nothing at all"]."

If they said something *adjacent* (but not a real answer), swap in:
> "They responded to ______________. Our claim was ______________. Those aren't the same thing — and the second one is still untouched."

## ② Rebuild the chain
> "That argument was: ______________ leads to ______________, because ______________. And that matters because it hits ______________, who ______________."

## ③ Name what the concession commits them to
> "By not contesting ______________, they've conceded ______________. That's a problem for them, because their own case on ______________ relies on ______________ — which they've now given up."

## ④ Foreclose the late answer
> "If you hear a response to this in their final speech, it's new — we get no chance to reply, so you shouldn't credit it."

## ⑤ Weigh it
> "So compare. Their best contested argument is ______________ — and because it's contested, at best you're ______% sure it happens. Ours is uncontested, so you're certain of it. It affects ______________ people, ______________ [more severely / sooner / irreversibly]. On that comparison alone, we win."

---

**One rule:** flag *one* strong drop and take it all the way through step ⑤ — don't cry "dropped" over every small point (it dilutes the word). A dropped argument, rebuilt and weighed, is the cheapest win on the board: certainty beats a contested maybe.
