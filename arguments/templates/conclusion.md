# Conclusion Template — "Land the plane"

Your most persistent unmet fix. Across Days 1, 2, and 4 you had **no real conclusion**;
Day 5 you finally *built* one (5:23) then talked past it and trailed off. So this is now a
**discipline** gap, not a skill gap. This file exists so you never wing the close again.

> **The core rule: WRITE YOUR LAST SENTENCE BEFORE YOU START SPEAKING.**
> During prep, the first two things you put on paper are your **crystallize line** and your
> **final callback** — *before* you draft a single argument. Then you physically cannot run
> out of runway. You always know where the plane lands.

---

## The 4 beats (~20–25 seconds, ONE conclusion, not two)

1. **Crystallize** — "This debate comes down to one question: ___."
   Frame the clash on *your* terms.
2. **Weigh** — one comparative sentence: *why you win that question*
   (name the axis: scope / magnitude / probability / reversibility / timeframe).
3. **Restate** — your two arguments as short parallel **labels, NOT re-explained paragraphs.**
   ("Stakeholders, turnout, proven." Let the evidence carry it.)
4. **Land** — call back your opening hook, then one clean declarative button. **Then STOP.**

---

## Worked model (your own voting-age material)

> *"This debate comes down to one question: should the people a law affects get a say in it?
> We've shown 16-year-olds are stakeholders politicians currently ignore, and that voting at
> 16 builds a habit that lasts a lifetime — both proven in Austria and Scotland. That
> outweighs any maturity worry, because a voice you never use is a voice you lose. At 16, you
> file a tax return. It's time you filed a ballot. Proud to propose."*

Crystallize → restate + evidence → weigh → **hook callback** → button. ~25 seconds. Notice it
does **not** re-explain each argument (Yoodli flagged your Day 5 recap as "long and mechanical").

## The fill-in-the-blank skeleton

> "This debate comes down to **[the one question]**.
> We've shown **[arg 1 label]** and **[arg 2 label]** — **[evidence callback in 3 words]**.
> That outweighs **[opp's best point]**, because **[our impact] beats theirs on [axis]**.
> **[Hook callback — echo your opening image].**
> Proud to propose."

Five blanks. Fill them during prep, before anything else.

---

## The three habits to break (from your transcripts)

1. **One conclusion, not two.** When you hit your crystallize line, you are *in* the close —
   land it and stop. Do **not** start "What have I done for you in my speech today?" (Day 5).
2. **Never trail off.** "So much more reasons" / "it is on that basis" / "because we care
   about those—" have no verb — they leak air. End on a declarative. *"Proud to propose."*
   is a clean button. Use it.
3. **Bring the hook home.** Non-negotiable. Whatever image you open with, its echo is your
   last sentence. (5 days running your best line was your *first* one, never reused.)
4. **The callback / button is the LITERAL last thing you say — nothing after it.** (Day 8·T2:
   you finally landed the drugs callback — "we take away the drugs because we prioritize our
   children over profit" — then tacked on "and it is on that basis I'm so, so proud" and it
   clipped before "to propose." You keep adding one clause after the button.) Decide your last
   **five words** in prep. Say them. Let go of the mic. Two clean patterns:
   - End on the callback: *"…they gave the kids the drug. We take it away. **Proud to propose.**"* [stop]
   - Or put "Proud to propose" first so the callback lands last: *"I'm proud to propose — because they gave the kids the drug, and **we take it away.**"* [stop]

---

## The drill

- **Record ONLY the last 20 seconds**, ten reps, always ending on the callback then silence.
  Groove the *stop*, not just the start.
- **Pre-write test:** before any timed case-write, write your crystallize line + final callback
  first. If they're not on the page before you draft arguments, the rep is set up to fail.

---

## Why this also fixes "writing fluently & interestingly" (your 5/10)
Your sentences are already good — the tax-form paradox, "a voice you never use is a voice you
lose." What collapses is the *landing*. Fluent speakers know their closing line before they open
their mouth. Nail the conclusion discipline and the style goal moves with it.
