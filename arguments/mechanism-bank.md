# Mechanism Bank

Pre-built causal chains, organized by motion theme. Drop these into Lucas's
counterfactual mechanism template.

## How to use
Each mechanism gives a **3-link chain on each side**, written as `A → B because C`:
- **Affirmative side:** Resolution → … → Outcome (3 links)
- **Counterfactual side:** No resolution → … → No outcome (3 links)

This expands the original 2-link template (res→mech, mech→outcome) into 3 links
for more granular, harder-to-break causation. Swap the bracketed `[slots]` for
the specific motion's actor/policy/resource. Add a **weighing sentence** after
each ("and this matters more than their argument because…").

> ⚠️ **Scaffolds, not scripts.** These are structures to *adapt*, never lines to recite. If a chain sounds memorised, you've used it wrong — judges punish canned delivery. The **weakest-link** tags are there to make you think, not parrot.

## ECONOMY

### Market Failure / Externality
**Aff (regulation → harm falls):**
1. Regulation → polluters face a price *because* the cost is legally enforced on every actor at once.
2. Price on harm → behavior changes *because* the harmful option is now dearer than the clean alternative.
3. Behavior change → harm falls *because* output of the harmful activity drops below the damaging threshold.

**CF (no regulation → harm persists):**
1. No regulation → the cost stays external *because* actors keep the profit while society absorbs the damage.
2. Externalized cost → no incentive to change *because* the harmful option stays cheapest for each actor.
3. No incentive → harm persists *because* rational profit-seekers won't restrain alone while rivals don't.

> **Weakest link:** Aff link 2 (price → behavior change). Opp attacks **elasticity** — if demand for the good is inelastic (fuel, medicine, addictive goods), a price rise just punishes consumers without changing behavior. Defend by showing real substitutes exist or the price bites hard enough (see tobacco-tax example).

> **They say → I say:**
> - *They say:* demand is inelastic, a price won't change behaviour. → *I say:* tobacco taxes cut smoking even though nicotine is addictive — and where a substitute exists (clean energy vs. fossil), the price bites faster and harder.
> - *They say:* it just hurts the poor. → *I say:* recycle the revenue as rebates to low-income households — the price signal stays, the burden doesn't.

### Incentive Distortion (tax / subsidy shifting behavior)
**Aff:**
1. Policy → the payoff of each choice shifts *because* a new cost or reward is attached to it.
2. New payoff → actors switch behavior *because* self-interest now points toward the desired action.
3. Mass switching → the market tips *because* competitive pressure forces even holdouts to follow.

**CF:**
1. No policy → old incentives stand *because* the cheaper/easier option is still the rational one.
2. Old incentives → behavior stays *because* no actor gains by moving first.
3. No movement → the status quo locks in *because* first-movers are punished, deterring change.

> **Weakest link:** Aff link 2 (new payoff → actors switch). Opp attacks **responsiveness** — big/rich actors can absorb the cost without changing, or find loopholes, so behavior doesn't actually flip. Defend by sizing the incentive to the actor and closing the obvious workaround.

### Inequality / Compounding Advantage
**Aff (redistribution → mobility):**
1. Redistribution → the poor gain access *because* resources reach those previously priced out.
2. Access → human capital rises *because* education and health investment become affordable.
3. Higher capital → mobility *because* skills convert into income across a lifetime.

**CF (no redistribution → inequality entrenches):**
1. No redistribution → gains stay concentrated *because* the wealthy capture returns on existing capital.
2. Concentration → advantage compounds *because* returns reinvest into more assets and influence.
3. Compounding → mobility freezes *because* each generation starts further apart.

> **Weakest link:** Aff link assumes redistribution **reaches the poor and doesn't kill growth**. Opp attacks the **efficiency–equity tradeoff** — redistribution can blunt the incentive to invest, or get captured by bureaucracy. Defend with the Scandinavia example (redistribution + growth coexist).

### Public Goods / Under-Provision  *(usually Prop — justifies state action)*
**Aff (state provides → the good exists):**
1. State provision → the good gets funded *because* government can compel contribution through taxes.
2. Funding → the good is built *because* resources now exist to provide and maintain it.
3. Provision → everyone benefits *because* the good is non-excludable (defense, clean air, basic research, public health).

**CF (no provision → under-provision):**
1. No state provision → private actors won't fund it *because* they can't capture the returns when free-riders use it without paying.
2. No funding → the good is under-provided *because* rational firms only build what they can profit from.
3. Under-provision → society loses *because* something everyone needs simply never gets made.

> **Weakest link:** Aff link 1 assumes **government provides efficiently**. Opp attacks waste, capture, or crowding out of private/charitable provision that would have happened anyway. Defend by showing the good genuinely *won't* appear privately (true public good).

### Crowding Out / Efficiency Cost  *(usually Opp — against spending/intervention)*
> ⚠️ **Label flip:** this chain is run by **Opposition**. Below, "Case side" = the motion happening (the intervention), and the *harm* is the outcome. Don't read it like the Prop-style mechanisms above.

**Case side (intervention → growth falls):**
1. Government borrows/taxes to fund the policy → private capital shrinks *because* the money is taken from or competes with private investment.
2. Less private capital → productivity growth slows *because* (the claim is) the private sector allocates capital more efficiently than the state.
3. Slower growth → everyone poorer long-term *because* a smaller economy means fewer jobs and lower wages.

**Counter side (no intervention → growth holds):**
1. No extra taxing/borrowing → private capital stays intact *because* it isn't displaced.
2. Intact capital → efficient investment continues *because* markets chase the highest returns.
3. Efficient investment → stronger growth *because* productive uses win out.

> **Weakest link:** Aff link 2 assumes **private allocation always beats public**. Prop attacks: markets misallocate (bubbles, short-termism, ignoring public goods), and public investment in infrastructure/R&D/education *raises* productivity rather than lowering it.

## RIGHTS

### Autonomy / Anti-Paternalism
**Aff (allow the choice → liberty protected):**
1. Allowing the choice → the individual self-governs *because* competent adults decide their own ends.
2. Self-governance → dignity *because* people live by their own values, not the majority's.
3. Dignity → a precedent of restraint *because* the state learns to justify limits by harm, not distaste.

**CF (ban → overreach):**
1. Banning the choice → the state imposes values *because* the majority's preference overrides the individual's.
2. Imposed values → a precedent of intrusion *because* the same logic licenses banning anything unpopular.
3. Intrusion precedent → liberty erodes *because* rights only matter when they hold against disapproval.

### Harm Principle (justifying a limit on a right)
**Aff (limit → third parties protected):**
1. Limiting the act → harm to others stops *because* the source of the injury is regulated.
2. Harm stops → victims' rights are restored *because* their freedom from injury outweighs the actor's preference.
3. Balanced rights → liberal consistency *because* every society already limits acts that injure non-consenting others.

**CF (no limit → harm continues):**
1. No limit → harm to others continues *because* the actor externalizes injury onto people who didn't consent.
2. Continued harm → victims bear the cost *because* they can't opt out of the imposed damage.
3. Unaddressed harm → the rights conflict is unresolved *because* one person's freedom cancels another's safety.

### Systemic Discrimination
**Aff (intervention → equal footing):**
1. Intervention → the neutral rule's bias is corrected *because* it's adjusted for unequal starting points.
2. Correction → access widens *because* historically excluded groups can now enter.
3. Wider access → the cycle breaks *because* representation builds the networks and wealth that were missing.

**CF (no intervention → discrimination persists):**
1. No intervention → the "neutral" rule keeps its bias *because* it encodes historical exclusion.
2. Hidden bias → the group stays behind *because* they lack the inherited advantage the rule assumes.
3. Staying behind → the gap compounds *because* each generation inherits the previous one's disadvantage.

## ENVIRONMENT

### Tragedy of the Commons
**Aff (binding rule → resource recovers):**
1. Binding rule → cost is enforced on all *because* no actor can defect without penalty.
2. Universal cost → overuse stops *because* restraint no longer just punishes the restrainer.
3. Overuse stops → the resource recovers *because* consumption falls below the renewal rate.

**CF (no rule → depletion):**
1. No rule → each actor offloads the cost *because* the resource is shared but the profit is private.
2. Offloading → a race to consume *because* waiting only lets rivals take the resource first.
3. Race → collapse *because* aggregate use exceeds what the resource can sustain.

### Intergenerational / Irreversibility
**Aff (act now → future protected):**
1. Acting now → emissions/damage are curbed *because* the harmful activity is reduced before thresholds.
2. Curbed damage → tipping points are avoided *because* the system stays within recoverable limits.
3. Recoverable system → the future inherits options *because* no irreversible loss is locked in.

**CF (delay → permanent loss):**
1. Delay → damage accumulates *because* the harmful activity continues unchecked.
2. Accumulation → a threshold is crossed *because* some ecological limits are non-linear and sudden.
3. Threshold crossed → the loss is permanent *because* extinctions and tipping points can't be reversed.

### Ladder-Pulling / Climate Justice (often Opposition)
> ⚠️ Usually run by **Opp** against uniform climate restrictions. "Aff" below = the *differentiated-burden* position the runner defends.

**Aff (differentiated burden → just *and* effective):**
1. Differentiated burden → developing nations keep developing *because* they aren't forced to halt growth.
2. Continued development → buy-in *because* they gain rather than sacrifice by joining.
3. Buy-in → global compliance *because* cooperation holds when the deal is seen as fair.

**CF (uniform restriction → failure):**
1. Uniform restriction → poor nations bear disproportionate cost *because* they can least afford to adapt.
2. Disproportionate cost → resentment and defection *because* the deal looks like ladder-pulling.
3. Defection → the agreement collapses *because* climate action needs the biggest emitters to stay in.

## INSTITUTIONS

### Capture / Accountability
**Aff (reform who funds/appoints → serves the public):**
1. Changing who funds/appoints → the institution answers to the public *because* its survival now depends on them.
2. Public accountability → decisions shift *because* conflicts resolve toward those affected.
3. Shifted decisions → legitimacy *because* the harmed finally have recourse.

**CF (status quo → capture persists):**
1. Status quo → the institution answers to its funders *because* they control its survival.
2. Funder control → biased decisions *because* conflicts resolve toward the powerful.
3. Bias → the harmed have no recourse *because* they have no voice in the structure.

### Coordination / Free-Riding
**Aff (institution → cooperation holds):**
1. Institution → defection carries a cost *because* monitoring and enforcement make cheating visible and punished.
2. Costly defection → states comply *because* cooperating now beats free-riding.
3. Compliance → the collective problem is solved *because* coordinated action reaches the needed scale.

**CF (no institution → collapse):**
1. No institution → defection is free *because* no one monitors or punishes cheating.
2. Free defection → everyone free-rides *because* restraint just lets others benefit at your expense.
3. Universal free-riding → the problem is unsolved *because* no actor supplies the public good alone.

### Legitimacy vs. Technocracy
**Aff (democratic control → durable policy):**
1. Democratic control → value trade-offs are chosen by the affected *because* those are political, not technical, questions.
2. Affected choosing → buy-in *because* people accept decisions they had a voice in.
3. Buy-in → policy endures *because* compliance rests on legitimacy, not just correctness.

**CF (technocratic control → fragile policy):**
1. Technocratic control → the affected are excluded *because* experts decide value questions for them.
2. Exclusion → no buy-in *because* people have no peaceful channel to dissent.
3. No buy-in → policy is fragile *because* even correct policy fails when seen as illegitimate.

## TECHNOLOGY & MEDIA

### Network Effects / Platform Monopoly
**Aff (regulate/open up → competition restored):**
1. Regulation → users can leave *because* interoperability and portability lower the switching cost.
2. Lower switching cost → rivals can compete *because* users aren't locked in by their network.
3. Competition → better outcomes *because* platforms must improve to retain users.

**CF (no regulation → monopoly entrenches):**
1. No regulation → users are locked in *because* everyone they need is on one platform.
2. Lock-in → no rival can enter *because* a new platform has no users and so no value.
3. No entry → the monopoly abuses its power *because* it faces no competitive check.

### Engagement Algorithms / Information Environment
**Aff (intervene → healthier discourse):**
1. Intervention → algorithms stop maximizing outrage *because* the incentive to amplify it is removed.
2. Less amplified outrage → exposure broadens *because* users aren't funneled into extremes.
3. Broader exposure → discourse moderates *because* people encounter and weigh opposing views.

**CF (status quo → polarization):**
1. Status quo → the algorithm maximizes engagement *because* that drives ad revenue.
2. Engagement-max → outrage is amplified *because* anger and fear are the most engaging content.
3. Amplified outrage → polarization *because* users are funneled into self-confirming extremes.

## CRIMINAL JUSTICE

### Deterrence
**Aff (credible penalty → crime falls):**
1. Credible penalty → the expected cost of crime rises *because* punishment is likely and known.
2. Higher expected cost → rational offenders abstain *because* crime no longer pays.
3. Abstention → crime falls *because* the marginal offender is deterred.

**CF (weak penalty → crime persists):**
1. Weak penalty → the expected cost stays low *because* punishment is unlikely or trivial.
2. Low cost → crime still pays *because* the gain outweighs the slim risk.
3. Crime pays → offending continues *because* the incentives are unchanged.

> Coach note: Opposition attacks link 2 — many crimes are impulsive or desperate, so
> offenders don't calculate cost. If that's true, the deterrence chain breaks at the second link.

### Rehabilitation vs. Recidivism
**Aff (rehab → reoffending falls):**
1. Rehabilitation → the root cause is addressed *because* addiction, lack of skills, or trauma is treated.
2. Cause addressed → reintegration is possible *because* the person can find legitimate work and stability.
3. Reintegration → recidivism falls *because* the driver of reoffending is removed.

**CF (punishment-only → cycle continues):**
1. Punishment-only → the root cause is untouched *because* prison addresses the act, not the cause.
2. Untouched cause → reentry fails *because* the released person faces the same conditions plus a record.
3. Failed reentry → reoffending *because* crime remains the only available path.

## INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS / SECURITY

### Deterrence / Security Dilemma
**Aff (de-escalation → stability):**
1. De-escalation → threat perception falls *because* neither side reads the other as preparing to attack.
2. Lower threat → the arms spiral stops *because* each side stops matching the other's buildup.
3. No spiral → conflict risk drops *because* miscalculation and the pressure to strike first decline.

**CF (escalation → instability):**
1. Buildup → the rival feels threatened *because* defensive moves look offensive from the outside.
2. Threatened rival → counter-buildup *because* it must match to feel safe.
3. Spiral → war risk rises *because* each step raises the stakes and shortens decision time.

### Soft Power / Norm-Setting
**Aff (lead by example → norm spreads):**
1. Setting the norm → others face pressure *because* deviating now carries a reputational cost.
2. Reputational cost → states conform *because* legitimacy and alliances depend on it.
3. Conformity → the norm becomes the default *because* widespread adherence makes it the expected standard.

**CF (no leadership → norm fails):**
1. No example → no standard exists *because* no credible actor models the behavior.
2. No standard → defection is costless *because* there's nothing to violate.
3. Costless defection → a race to the bottom *because* each actor does whatever serves its short-term interest.
